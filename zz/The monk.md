**The Timeline:**  
Everything happens roughly at the same time in the novel. The Raymond/Agnes story and the Lorenzo/Antonia story are running parallel, and they intersect because Lorenzo is Raymond's friend AND Agnes's brother.

**Agnes's backstory - why she became a nun:**  
You're right to pick up on this! Agnes didn't choose to become a nun. Her parents forced her into the convent to prevent her marriage to Raymond. Here's what happened:

Raymond and Agnes fell in love and wanted to marry, but Agnes's family (including Lorenzo initially) opposed the match. They thought Raymond wasn't good enough for her or something like that. To make sure Agnes couldn't marry Raymond, her parents literally forced her into the convent of St. Clare to become a nun against her will.

But Agnes and Raymond were really in love, so they continued their affair in secret even after she was forced to take her vows. Raymond would sneak into the convent to see her (disguised as a gardener, if I remember correctly). That's how she got pregnant - they were still seeing each other secretly because their love was genuine and she never wanted to be a nun in the first place.

This is actually one of the novel's criticisms of the Catholic Church - forcing women into convents against their will, which Lewis shows leads to tragedy. Agnes is a victim of her family's tyranny and the church's cruel punishment when she's discovered.

**About Rosario/Matilda:**  
Just to clarify - Rosario/Matilda isn't connected to Raymond at all. She's the woman who disguised herself as a male novice monk (Rosario) to get close to Ambrosio, then reveals herself as Matilda and becomes his lover and accomplice. She's a completely separate character from the Raymond/Agnes storyline.

The novel is really about how these two corrupt religious figures (Ambrosio the monk and the Prioress of St. Clare) destroy innocent lives while pretending to be holy, with supernatural/demonic forces involved in Ambrosio's fall from grace.

**The Bleeding Nun story:**

This is actually a separate subplot earlier in the novel! It's a ghost story that happens to Raymond:

Raymond is trying to elope with Agnes from her family's castle in Germany. The plan is that Agnes will dress as "the Bleeding Nun" - a legendary ghost that supposedly haunts the castle and appears at certain times. This way, when the guards see a figure in a nun's bloody habit, they'll think it's the ghost and let her pass.

But here's the twist - the _actual_ ghost of the Bleeding Nun appears instead of Agnes! Raymond accidentally elopes with a real ghost. He doesn't realize it at first, but then strange things start happening:

- She appears to him every night at midnight
- She's ice cold to touch
- She tells him her tragic backstory

The Bleeding Nun was actually Beatrice de las Cisternas, who was murdered long ago. She was a wicked noblewoman who had committed terrible crimes (including murder). She can't rest until her bones are properly buried and someone fulfills certain conditions.

Raymond is literally haunted by her - she appears every night and he starts wasting away. Finally, he meets the Wandering Jew (yes, that legendary figure), who helps him:

- They find her bones
- Give her a proper burial
- Perform the right religious ceremonies
- This finally frees Raymond from her haunting

## Does the book explain **why** the Bleeding Nun was killed?

In _The Monk_ by Matthew Lewis (the novel where the Bleeding Nun appears), **the exact motive for her murder is never fully explained**. The author **deliberately leaves it vague**.

## What the book _does_ tell us

The Bleeding Nun‚Äôs story is given as a **brief legend**, not a detailed biography. From the text, we know that:

- She **broke her religious vows**
- She **planned to escape the convent at night**
- She **died violently** during that escape
- Her body was **left unburied**
- As punishment, she must **wander at night, bleeding**

However:

- The book **does not clearly say** who killed her
- It **does not say** whether it was her lover, another person, or an accident
- It **does not give a moral explanation**, like jealousy or anger

So any specific explanation (for example, ‚Äúher lover killed her‚Äù) is **interpretation**, not confirmed fact.

---

## Why the author leaves it unclear

This was very intentional.

Matthew Lewis uses the Bleeding Nun as:

- A **Gothic symbol**, not a realistic character
- A warning about **breaking vows**
- A way to create **mystery and unease**

In Gothic literature, especially in the late 1700s, writers often:

- Avoided practical details
- Focused more on **punishment and atmosphere**
- Let readers **fill in the gaps themselves**

The horror comes from **not knowing**, not from clear logic.
