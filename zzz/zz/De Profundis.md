## Why Wilde Wrote It

Wilde wrote _De Profundis_ because:

1. He had **nothing else left**
2. He was **alone**
3. He had lost:
    - His freedom
    - His career
    - His reputation
    - His family
4. He was forced to **face himself** for the first time

Before prison, Wilde lived for:

- Pleasure
- Beauty
- Wit
- Applause
- Being admired

In prison, all of that was gone.

So he wrote this letter to:

- Understand his pain
- Understand love
- Understand suffering
- Understand who he really was

## The Tone of the Book

The tone changes a lot.

At different moments, Wilde sounds:

- Angry
- Hurt
- Bitter
- Loving
- Wise
- Calm
- Broken
- Forgiving

Sometimes he blames Bosie.  
Sometimes he blames himself.  
Sometimes he accepts everything quietly.

That change is important—it shows **real emotional growth**, not fake wisdom.

---

## Main Ideas in _De Profundis_

Let’s go through the **big ideas**, one by one.

---

### 1. Love That Turned Into Pain

Wilde loved Bosie, but that love:

- Cost him his money
- Cost him his peace
- Cost him his safety
- Eventually cost him his freedom

He explains how Bosie:

- Demanded constant attention
- Loved drama and fights
- Never listened
- Never took responsibility

Wilde admits something very honest:

> Love can exist and still destroy you.

He realizes that **love without kindness and balance becomes harmful**.

---

### 2. Responsibility and Self-Blame

At first, Wilde sounds angry at Bosie.

But slowly, he admits something deeper:

- No one forced him to stay
- No one forced him to spend money
- No one forced him to ignore warnings

He accepts that:

> I made choices, and those choices led me here.

This is one of the most mature parts of the book.

He stops being a victim and becomes **accountable**.

---

### 3. The Meaning of Suffering

Before prison, Wilde believed:

- Pleasure was the highest goal
- Pain was useless
- Life should be beautiful and joyful

Prison destroyed that belief.

In _De Profundis_, he comes to a new understanding:

- Suffering can teach
- Pain can soften the heart
- Loss can deepen the soul

He doesn’t say suffering is good.  
He says suffering **changes you**, whether you want it or not.

And if you accept it, it can make you:

- More humble
- More honest
- More human

---

### 4. Humility (A Huge Change for Wilde)

Oscar Wilde was once proud, famous, and admired.

In prison:

- He wore the same clothes as everyone
- He followed strict rules
- He was ignored and controlled

At first, this crushed him.

Later, he realizes:

> Pride kept me shallow. Humility makes me real.

This is a massive shift for someone once known for ego and brilliance.

---

### 5. Christ as a Symbol (Not Religion)

Wilde talks a lot about **Jesus Christ**, but not in a church way.

He sees Christ as:

- Someone rejected
- Someone misunderstood
- Someone who suffered without hate

To Wilde, Christ represents:

- Compassion
- Forgiveness
- Quiet strength
- Emotional truth

This helps Wilde accept his own suffering without becoming cruel.

---

### 6. Forgiveness (Not Forgetting)

By the end of the letter, Wilde moves toward forgiveness.

Not because Bosie deserves it.  
Not because the pain wasn’t real.

But because:

> Holding hatred would keep him imprisoned even after prison.

Forgiveness becomes **freedom of the heart**, not approval of harm.

---

## What _De Profundis_ Is Really About

At its core, _De Profundis_ is about:

- Losing everything
- Facing yourself without masks
- Understanding love honestly
- Accepting pain without denial
- Growing through humiliation
- Learning compassion

It is the story of a man **becoming deeper because life broke him open**.

---

## Why the Book Still Matters Today

People still read _De Profundis_ because:

- It talks honestly about toxic love
- It shows emotional growth without pretending
- It captures depression, regret, and self-reflection perfectly
- It reminds us that suffering can reshape us—not erase us

It feels modern because **human pain hasn’t changed**.

Why only him??

**Bosie wasn’t arrested because the law, power, and social class protected him — and Oscar Wilde was the easier target.**

Now let’s walk through it properly.

---

## 1. What the Law Actually Targeted

At that time in England (late 1800s):

- Being gay itself wasn’t named clearly in the law
- Instead, the crime was called **“gross indecency”**
- It was enforced **selectively**, not fairly

The law mostly went after:

- Older men
- Famous men
- Men without powerful family protection

So enforcement was **uneven**, not equal.

---

## 2. Bosie’s Powerful Shield: His Family

This is the biggest reason.

Bosie was:

- Young
- Rich
- From an **extremely powerful aristocratic family**

His father was:

- The **Marquess of Queensberry**
- A man with influence, money, and political weight

Oscar Wilde was:

- Famous, yes
- But **socially vulnerable**
- Dependent on his reputation to survive

When things exploded, **Bosie had a safety net**. Wilde didn’t.

---

## 3. Who Started the Legal Disaster (This Part Is Crucial)

Here’s the tragic irony:

- Bosie pushed Wilde to **sue Bosie’s father for libel**
- Wilde listened
- That lawsuit **backfired completely**

During the trial:

- Wilde’s private life was exposed
- Evidence of same‑sex relationships was presented
- The state stepped in

Result:

- The case against Queensberry collapsed
- Wilde was arrested
- Bosie walked away untouched

So Wilde wasn’t hunted randomly — he was **dragged into the system by a legal trap**.

---

## 4. Why Bosie Was Not Prosecuted

You’re absolutely right to ask:

> “If both were involved, why only one punished?”

Here’s why:

### a. The law needed a **public example**

Wilde was:

- Older
- Famous
- Seen as “corrupting youth” (a common accusation then)

Bosie was framed as:

- A misguided young aristocrat
- A “victim,” not an offender

That’s unfair — but that’s how power works.

---

### b. Class bias (this is blunt, but true)

Justice then was not blind.

- Rich + noble = protection
- Famous but dependent = disposable

Bosie could disappear into family protection.  
Wilde could not.

---

### c. Evidence and strategy

- Prosecutors focused on Wilde
- Going after Bosie would have:
    - Exposed elite families
    - Created political trouble
    - Shaken the upper class

So they **didn’t want that mess**.

---

## 5. Was Bosie “Innocent”? No.

Even by the standards of the time:

- Bosie encouraged risky behavior
- He relied on Wilde to take the danger
- He did not step in to protect Wilde

But innocence and **legal safety** are not the same thing.

---

## 6. How Wilde Saw This (Important for _De Profundis_)

In _De Profundis_, Wilde slowly realizes:

- Bosie didn’t save him
- Bosie didn’t share the consequences
- Bosie continued his life while Wilde suffered

That realization is part of Wilde’s pain — and part of why the letter exists.

But Wilde also admits:

> “I chose this path. I wasn’t forced.”

That’s how he balances blame and responsibility.

---

## 7. So You’re Right — Morally, Yes. Legally, No.

**Morally:**

- Yes, both were involved
- Yes, both should have been accountable

**Legally and socially (at that time):**

- Only Wilde was vulnerable
- Only Wilde was punished

It wasn’t justice.  
It was **power protecting itself**.

# Reunion ??
---

## 1. Who Initiated the Reunion?

**Both men did — but Bosie pushed harder.**

After Wilde’s release from prison in 1897:

- **Wilde was advised by everyone** (friends, lawyers, doctors) to _stay away from Bosie_
- Bosie, however:
    - **Wrote repeatedly** to Wilde
    - Pressured him emotionally
    - Framed their reunion as a matter of loyalty and love

Wilde, lonely, disgraced, and emotionally shattered by prison, **gave in**.

> In short:  
> **Bosie pursued the reunion; Wilde consented out of need and weakness**, not strength.

---

## 2. Why the Reunion Made Wilde’s Life Worse

### A. Financial Disaster

Bosie had **expensive tastes and no money**.

- He expected:
    - Comfortable hotels
    - Travel
    - Dining out
- Wilde had:
    - Very little income
    - No stable publishing revenue
- Result:
    - Wilde spent money he didn’t have
    - Borrowed constantly
    - Deepened his poverty

Instead of stability, Wilde got **financial hemorrhaging**.

---

### B. Emotional and Psychological Damage

The relationship had always been volatile, but after prison it became worse:

- Bosie:
    - Was **temperamental and argumentative**
    - Blamed others (including Wilde) for past events
    - Showed little remorse for the trial’s consequences
- Wilde:
    - Was physically weak and emotionally vulnerable
    - Needed peace, routine, and healing

The reunion **reopened wounds** rather than soothing them.

---

### C. Social and Legal Risk

This is crucial.

- Wilde was still:
    - **Legally vulnerable**
    - Under intense public scrutiny
- Being publicly associated with Bosie:
    - Rekindled scandal
    - Alarmed supporters who were trying to protect Wilde
    - Risked renewed legal or police attention

Friends who were helping Wilde **withdrew support** when Bosie reappeared.

---

### D. Isolation from Supporters

Many of Wilde’s loyal friends issued an ultimatum:

> _We will help you — but not if Bosie is in your life._

Wilde chose Bosie — briefly — and lost:

- Financial assistance
- Emotional support
- Practical help (housing, medical care, connections)

This isolation was devastating.

---

### E. No Creative Recovery

Bosie claimed he wanted Wilde to write again — but:

- The constant conflict and instability **prevented sustained work**
- Wilde produced little during this period
- His health declined faster under stress

Instead of being a muse, Bosie was a **distraction and drain**.

---

## 3. Why Did Bosie Want the Reunion?

Bosie’s motives were complex and not entirely noble:

### Possible Reasons (Not Mutually Exclusive)

- **Guilt** — but without responsibility  
    He felt bad about Wilde’s suffering, yet refused to accept his own role.
- **Control**  
    Bosie was used to dominating the relationship.
- **Romantic obsession**  
    He idealized their love story and ignored reality.
- **Defiance**  
    He wanted to prove that society had not “won.”

What’s striking is what Bosie _didn’t_ want:

- He did not want quiet
- He did not want restraint
- He did not want Wilde to heal apart from him

---

## 4. Why Wilde Agreed (Despite Knowing Better)

Wilde wasn’t naïve — he was **emotionally wrecked**.

After prison, he was:

- Humiliated
- Lonely
- Physically ill
- Estranged from his children
- Cut off from English society

Bosie represented:

- Familiar affection
- A link to his former life
- Emotional intensity when everything else felt numb

In a tragic irony, **Wilde chose emotional comfort over survival**.

---

## 5. How the Reunion Ended

- The reunion lasted only a few months
- Financial pressure and constant fighting tore it apart
- Bosie’s family intervened and cut off funds
- Wilde was left **poorer, sicker, and more isolated**

After this, Wilde’s decline accelerated toward his death in 1900.
